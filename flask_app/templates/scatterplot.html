To drive, or not to drive, that is the question.

Having taken a look at the average tickets issued per weather types, we decided to dive deeper into other weather metrics in the weather dataset to see if we can find any correlations.

To do that, we built a scatter plot that shows various weather metrics on the x-axis and parking metrics on the y-axis. Again, each dot is a complete hour with a weather type recorded for that time period. The majority of the metrics are implemented to explore different combinations. Using rainy conditions and their precipitation volumes against total ticket count as a case study, we do not see a strong correlation. The high ticket average shown for the "very heavy rain" weather type is also plotted on the chart (far right). Despite that data point showed a whopping 111 tickets collected under an extremely inclement weather condition (26.7mm precipitation volume) within an hour, we would likely consider it as an outlier that should not have an significant impact on our analysis. Nevertheless, it looks like when the precipitation volume exceeded 5mm the ticket count tended to remain on the lower end. There could potentially be a correlation here but a dataset of more than just a 30% sample of the population needs to be collected and further analysis needs to be performed to make a definitive conclusion.

Having tried to visualize these data points on a scatter plot, while they are certainly pleasing on the eyes, we believe other frameworks such as using histograms to show data distribution or calculating average hourly ticket issurance for each incremental range of a specific metric (e.g. precipitation volume: 1mm, 2mm, 3mm, etc.) like we have done in our "Statistics" page could potentially provide us more accurate insights. Given the time, these are excellent action items for further improving our analysis.
